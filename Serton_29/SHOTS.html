Serton 29: Mother's Abortion

Vienna, Austria - Hospital procedure, 1947

Setting

Vienna hospital, surgical procedure room

Emotional Tone

Difficult necessity, child's fear for mother, mistrust of German doctors, survival pragmatism vs. moral unease

Key Action

Mother becomes pregnant during displacement journey, chooses abortion due to poor health, child narrator fears German doctors will harm her

Shot Count

5 shots total

Emotional Arc

Opens with the difficult reality of mother's pregnancy during displacement, reveals her decision to have an abortion given her fragile health and circumstances, captures the child narrator's complex emotions - understanding the necessity but not feeling happy about it, deepens into his profound mistrust and fear of German doctors, and concludes with his terror that they might harm his mother - showing how Holocaust trauma created lasting suspicion even in necessary medical care.

Historical Context

Pregnancy during displacement was common and often unwanted - many women became pregnant during chaotic border crossings, in DP camps, or during the early post-war period. Abortion was available in Vienna hospitals in 1947, though still carried social stigma. For Holocaust survivors, especially those in fragile health from years of starvation and trauma, pregnancy posed serious medical risks. The profound mistrust of German medical personnel was entirely justified - Jewish survivors knew about Nazi medical experiments, forced sterilizations, and medical murder. Having to trust German doctors with intimate medical procedures required overcoming deep trauma and required immense courage.
